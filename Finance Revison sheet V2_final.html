Section 01 · The Big Picture
Why Do Financial Statements Exist?
Core Insight
🏠 Think of a business like a household. Just like you track your salary, savings, and bills — a company tracks its money through financial statements. They answer three questions: Where did the money come from? Where did it go? Where is it now?
👔 The Manager's Job: Managers are decision-makers. Every decision requires choosing between alternatives and making the best use of limited resources. Financial statements are the manager's scoreboard — they show how well resources were deployed.
The 4 Financial Statements
📊 Balance Sheet
A photograph of the company at one moment in time — what it owns, what it owes, and what's left for owners.
Like checking your bank account + property value on a specific date.
📈 Income Statement
A video of the company over a period — how much was earned and spent.
Like your monthly salary slip showing earnings and deductions.
💸 Cash Flow Statement
Tracks the actual movement of cash — money coming in vs. going out.
Profit on paper ≠ cash in hand. This shows the difference.
📋 Shareholders' Equity
Shows how the owners' stake in the company has changed over time.
Like tracking how your personal net worth grew or shrank over a year.
Section 02 · Resource Statement
The Balance Sheet
The Golden Rule
The Fundamental Equation
ASSETS = LIABILITIES + SHAREHOLDERS' EQUITY
Everything a company owns must be funded either by borrowing (liabilities) or by owner money (equity). Always balanced. Always.
🏡 House Analogy: You bought a ₹50L house. You paid ₹20L yourself (your equity) and borrowed ₹30L from the bank (liability). The house (asset = ₹50L) = Your money (₹20L) + Bank loan (₹30L). Balanced!
The Three Parts
🏭 Assets (Left Side)
Everything the company owns or controls that has value. Ordered from most liquid (easiest to convert to cash) to least liquid.
Current Assets → convert to cash within 1 year (inventory, receivables, cash)
Non-Current Assets → long-term (land, machinery, goodwill)
💳 Liabilities (Right Side)
Money the company owes to others — banks, suppliers, employees, government.
Current Liabilities → payable within 1 year
Non-Current Liabilities → payable after 1 year (long-term loans)
👥 Shareholders' Equity
The leftover after paying all liabilities — this belongs to the owners/shareholders. Also called "net worth" or "book value".
If the company sold everything and paid all debts, equity is what's left. Think of it as the owners' share of the business.
✨ Goodwill (Special Asset)
The extra premium paid when acquiring another company — value of brand, reputation, customer base beyond physical assets.
ITC buys Company X (book value ₹100Cr) for ₹150Cr → ₹50Cr appears as Goodwill on ITC's balance sheet.

The Income Statement (P&L)
What It Is
🪜 Think of it as a staircase going downward. You start at the top with all the money earned (Revenue), and at each step you subtract various costs. What's left at the very bottom — after all deductions — is called Net Profit (PAT). This is the "bottom line" everyone talks about.
The Staircase — From Revenue to Net Profit
🔝 Revenue from Operations ("Top Line") ITC: ₹76,840 Cr
↓ minus Cost of Materials & Production
= Gross Profit Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold
↓ minus Operating Expenses (Salaries, Rent, Marketing…)
= Operating Profit (EBIT) Profit from core operations
↓ minus Interest & Tax
⬇ Net Profit / PAT ("Bottom Line") ITC: ₹20,751 Cr
ITC's P&L in one line: ITC earned ₹76,840 Cr in revenue, spent ₹52,448 Cr in costs, paid ₹6,388 Cr in taxes, and kept ₹20,751 Cr as profit. That profit belongs to shareholders — which is what drives the stock price.
Key Terms in the Income Statement
Revenue / Top Line
Total money earned from selling goods or services before any costs are removed.
ITC's cigarettes, foods, hotels, paperboards all together = ₹76,840 Cr
COGS (Cost of Goods Sold)
Direct cost to produce what was sold — raw materials, manufacturing labour, factory costs.
Tobacco leaf, wheat flour, packaging — whatever goes directly into making the product.
Depreciation & Amortisation
Machinery and equipment wear out over time. Depreciation spreads the cost of an asset over its useful life — even though no cash is spent each year.
A ₹10Cr machine lasting 10 years = ₹1Cr depreciation charged each year. ITC: ₹1,816 Cr.
PAT (Profit After Tax) / Net Profit
The final take-home profit after all expenses and taxes are paid. Divided among shareholders or reinvested.
ITC's PAT = ₹20,751 Cr. This is the "bottom line" that investors care about most.
EPS (Earnings Per Share)
PAT ÷ Total shares outstanding. Tells you how much profit belongs to each share you hold.
ITC EPS = ₹16.42 (Basic). So each ₹1 face-value share earns ₹16.42 in profit for the year.
Other Comprehensive Income
Gains/losses that bypass the main P&L — e.g. revaluation of investments, foreign exchange translation. These still affect equity.
ITC had ₹2,957 Cr of equity investment gains here — not in "normal" profit but still real value.

Section 04
Cash Flow Statement
Why Cash Flow ≠ Profit
🧾 The classic trap: A shop sells ₹10L worth of goods on credit (the buyer hasn't paid yet). The Income Statement shows ₹10L profit. The cash account shows ₹0 received. Which one helps pay salaries? Cash does. That's why we need the Cash Flow Statement separately.
The Three Buckets of Cash Flow
⚙️
Operating Activities
Cash from running the day-to-day business — selling products, collecting from customers, paying suppliers and employees.
🏗️
Investing Activities
Cash spent or received from buying/selling long-term assets — factories, equipment, investments. Usually negative (spending).
🏦
Financing Activities
Cash raised or returned from owners and lenders — issuing shares, taking/repaying loans, paying dividends.
Rule of thumb for healthy companies: Operating Cash Flow should be positive (business generates cash). Investing Cash Flow is usually negative (growing by spending on assets). Financing can go either way.
Non-Cash Items: The Reconciliation Trick
🔄 The cash flow statement starts with net profit and adds back depreciation. Why? Because depreciation is an expense on the P&L that reduces profit — but no cash actually left the company. So we add it back to get to true cash generated. ITC added back ₹1,816 Cr of depreciation this way.

Section 05 · Valuation Ratios
P/E and P/B — Are You Overpaying?
The Core Question
🛒 If a shop is earning ₹1 lakh/month, how much would you pay to buy it? ₹10L? ₹25L? That's what valuation ratios answer for stock markets — how much are investors paying relative to what the company earns or owns?
P/E and P/B Side by Side
📈 P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
P/E = Market Price per Share ÷ EPS
Tells you: "How many rupees am I paying for every ₹1 of the company's earnings?"

P/E = 25 → You're paying ₹25 to earn ₹1 annually.
Higher P/E = more expensive / higher growth expectations.

Used for: Profitable companies in most industries.
ITC Market P/E ≈ 22× | Calculated P/E = 420 ÷ 16.42 = 25.6×
📚 P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)
P/B = Market Price per Share ÷ Book Value per Share
Book Value = what the company is worth on paper (Total Assets − Total Liabilities).

P/B = 3 → You're paying ₹3 for every ₹1 of the company's net assets.
P/B < 1 = stock may be undervalued.

Preferred for: Banks & financial companies.
ITC Market P/B ≈ 4× | Calculated P/B = 420 ÷ 60 = 7.0×

Step by Step
BVPS = Total Shareholders' Equity ÷ Total Shares Outstanding

Other Key Ratios (Quick Reference)
⚖️ Debt-to-Equity Ratio
D/E = Total Liabilities ÷ Shareholders' Equity
D/E = 2 → Company has ₹2 of debt for every ₹1 of owner money. Lower is safer. ITC's D/E is near zero — almost no debt.
💰 Working Capital
Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
Positive = company can pay short-term bills. ITC: ₹41,066 − ₹13,690 = ₹27,376 Cr surplus. Very healthy.
📦 Inventory Turnover
= Cost of Sales ÷ Average Inventory
Ratio of 5 = inventory converts to sales 5 times a year. Higher is usually better — means goods aren't sitting unsold.
📊 Operating Margin
= Operating Income ÷ Net Revenue × 100
Shows what % of each sales rupee is actual operating profit. Higher = more efficient. Compare within same industry only.

ROE & Profitability Analysis
Why Profitability Analysis?
🎯 Every investor evaluates a company through four lenses: Return, Risk, Growth (CAGR of revenue), and Sustainability. Profitability analysis is the systematic way of answering the Return question — and it starts with ROE.
🏭 The Revenue Claimants Chain: When a company earns revenue, it has to pay people in this order: Suppliers → Employees → Banks (interest) → Tax Authority → Preference Shareholders → and finally, whatever's left goes to Equity Shareholders. ROE measures how well equity shareholders were rewarded after everyone else was paid.
The Three Core Return Metrics
📈 ROE — Return on Equity
How much profit was generated for every rupee the shareholders invested. The shareholder's scorecard.
ROE = PAT ÷ Equity
ITC: ₹20,751 ÷ ₹74,889 = 27%
Shareholders earn ₹27 for every ₹100 they invested.
🏗️ ROA — Return on Assets
How much profit was generated from every rupee of total assets — regardless of whether funded by debt or equity. Measures management efficiency.
ROA = Net Income ÷ Total Assets
ITC: ₹20,751 ÷ ₹91,826 = 22.6%
Every ₹100 of assets generated ₹22.6 in profit.
🏦 ROCE — Return on Capital Employed
Return generated on all the capital deployed — equity plus debt. Used by managers and bankers (not just shareholders).
ROCE = EBIT ÷ (Equity + Debt)
ITC: ₹27,147 ÷ ₹75,300 = 36%
Use NOPAT (PAT + Interest × (1−tax)) for precision.
💡 ROE vs ROCE — Which to Use?
Depends on whose eyes you're looking through.
Shareholder viewpoint → Use ROE
Manager / Banker viewpoint → Use ROCE

ROE only counts equity cost; ROCE counts all capital cost.
EBITDA — The Cross-Company Comparator
⚖️ Why not just compare PAT? Two companies may have identical operations but different tax regimes, different debt levels, or different depreciation policies — making their PATs incomparable. EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortisation) strips all of that out, leaving only the core operational performance.
EBITDA Breakdown
EBITDA = Revenue − Operating Expenses (excl. D&A)
ITC: Revenue ₹76,840 − Op. Expenses ₹50,586 = EBITDA ≈ ₹26,254 Cr
Rule: When comparing two companies, use EBITDA (not PAT) because PAT is distorted by each company's tax rate, debt level, and accounting choices for depreciation. EBITDA shows the "true" operating engine.
CAGR — Measuring Growth Rate
📈 CAGR (Compounded Annual Growth Rate) is the smoothed annual growth rate — as if the company grew at a steady pace each year. It removes year-to-year volatility and shows the underlying trend. Example from class (TCS): Revenue CAGR over 10 years = 10%, last 5 years = 12%, last year = 6% — the deceleration signals something worth investigating.
CAGR Formula
CAGR = (Ending Value ÷ Beginning Value)^(1/n) − 1
n = number of years. Result expressed as a percentage.

DuPont Analysis — Dissecting ROE
The Core Idea
🔬 Imagine your car's fuel efficiency dropped. You wouldn't just say "the car is bad" — you'd check: Is it the engine (profit margin)? The weight (assets)? The fuel type (leverage)? DuPont Analysis does exactly this for ROE — it breaks it into three parts so you can pinpoint exactly why ROE is high or low.
Step-by-Step DuPont Derivation
Start: ROE = Net Income ÷ Equity ITC: 20,751 ÷ 74,889 = 27%
↓ Multiply by Assets/Assets (= 1, changes nothing)
ROE = (Net Income ÷ Assets) × (Assets ÷ Equity) = ROA × Equity Multiplier
↓ Multiply by Sales/Sales (= 1, changes nothing)
ROE = (Sales ÷ Assets) × (Net Income ÷ Sales) × (Assets ÷ Equity) = TATO × PM × EM
The 3-Factor DuPont Formula
3-Factor DuPont (Classic)
ROE = TATO × Profit Margin × Equity Multiplier
ROE = (Sales÷Assets) × (Net Income÷Sales) × (Assets÷Equity)
⚙️ TATO — Total Asset Turnover
Sales generated per rupee of assets. Measures how efficiently assets are being used to generate revenue.
TATO = Sales ÷ Assets
ITC: ₹76,840 ÷ ₹91,826 = 0.84×
Low TATO is normal for asset-heavy companies.
💰 PM — Profit Margin
How much profit survives from each rupee of sales after all costs. Measures pricing power and cost control.
PM = Net Income ÷ Sales
ITC: ₹20,751 ÷ ₹76,840 = 27%
High PM means strong brand / pricing power.
🏦 EM — Equity Multiplier
How much of the asset base is funded by equity. Higher EM = more leverage (debt used). Financial lever.
EM = Assets ÷ Equity
ITC: ₹91,826 ÷ ₹74,889 = 1.23×
ITC's EM is very low — almost no debt leverage.
🔢 ITC DuPont Check
Multiply all three factors — should equal ROE.
TATO × PM × EM
= 0.84 × 0.27 × 1.23
= ≈ 27% ✓ (matches ROE)
What to Do When ROE is Too Low?
Factor Low?Root CauseSolutions
📉 TATO too low Not getting enough revenue from assets — under-utilised capacity Price discounts, better advertising, new products, expand distribution
📉 PM too low Revenue comes in but leaks out as costs — poor cost control or weak pricing Renegotiate with suppliers, reduce COGS, improve employee efficiency, premium pricing
📉 EM too low Capital structure not optimised — company may be too equity-heavy and under-leveraged Increase debt use, repurchase shares, reduce retained earnings (pay more dividends)

5-Factor DuPont — Going Deeper
🔭 The 3-factor model groups all profitability into "Profit Margin." The 5-factor model splits that margin into: Operating Efficiency + Non-Operating Items + Tax Efficiency. This is more useful when comparing companies with different tax rates or significant non-operating income.
5-Factor DuPont
ROE = Leverage × TATO × Operating Margin × Non-Op Ratio × Tax Effect
(Assets÷Equity) × (Sales÷Assets) × (EBIT÷Sales) × (EBT÷EBIT) × (PAT÷EBT)
Operating Margin
EBIT ÷ Revenue. Profit from pure operations before interest/tax. Captures core business efficiency.
ITC: ₹27,147 ÷ ₹76,840 = 35.3% operating margin
Non-Operating Ratio
EBT ÷ EBIT. Shows impact of interest expense. If <1, the company is paying significant interest costs.
ITC: ~₹27,140 ÷ ₹27,147 ≈ 1.0 — virtually no interest drag (almost no debt)
Tax Effect
PAT ÷ EBT = (1 − effective tax rate). Shows how much of pre-tax profit survives taxation.
ITC: PAT ₹20,751 ÷ PBT ₹27,140 = 76.5% retained → effective tax rate ≈ 23.5%
Why Use 5-Factor?
When two companies look similar on 3-factor, the 5-factor reveals hidden differences in tax efficiency or interest burden.
Comparing Infosys vs ITC: Infosys has minimal assets but high margins; ITC has high assets but also high margins — 5-factor makes this visible.

Growth Rates — How Fast Can a Company Grow?
The Retention Ratio — Fuel for Growth
🌱 A company earns profit. It can either pay it out as dividends (shareholders take cash) or keep it (retained earnings reinvested into the business). The fraction kept is the retention ratio — it's the fuel that powers internal growth.
Retention Ratio (b)
b = 1 − Payout Ratio = 1 − (DPS ÷ EPS)
DPS = Dividend Per Share · EPS = Earnings Per Share · b = fraction of profit kept
Two Types of Growth Rate
🏠 Internal Growth Rate
= (ROA × b) ÷ (1 − ROA × b)
What it means: The maximum rate the company can grow using only its own profits — no new external debt or equity raised.

Analogy: Like growing a business purely by reinvesting your own salary — no bank loans, no investors.

Most restrictive growth estimate. Good baseline for conservative planning.
🚀 Sustainable Growth Rate
= (ROE × b) ÷ (1 − ROE × b)
What it means: The maximum rate the company can grow while: (1) not issuing new equity, and (2) keeping the same debt-to-equity ratio.

Analogy: You reinvest profits AND take on proportionally more bank debt — but keep the balance between your own money and borrowed money the same.

Always higher than internal growth rate because ROE > ROA for any leveraged firm.
Key Difference: Internal Growth Rate uses ROA (all assets, no new funding). Sustainable Growth Rate uses ROE (equity base grows, debt grows proportionally alongside). The only formula difference is ROA vs ROE in the numerator — but the implication is significant.
Why Growth Rates Matter for Investors
Forecasting shareholder value requires forecasting profitability (ROE) and growth (sustainable rate). These two together — not just current P/E — determine whether a stock is truly cheap or expensive.
Sustainable growth rate is used in dividend discount models to normalise long-term growth assumptions — especially for years beyond the 5-year detailed forecast period.
A company growing faster than its sustainable rate is either borrowing more (increasing leverage risk), diluting shareholders (issuing new shares), or both. Watch out.

Cautions When Using Ratios
Same period, same accounting policies: Only compare companies using the same depreciation method, inventory valuation, and reporting period. Different policies make ratios misleading.

Look beyond the numbers: Read footnotes, understand accounting policies, and consult the MD&A. Ratios without context are dangerous — a company can manipulate earnings while ratios look fine.

WACC — The Hurdle Rate Every Project Must Clear
The Core Idea
🏃 Every business gets its money from two sources — owners (equity) and banks (debt). Both have a cost. WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital) is the blended cost of all the capital a company uses. Think of it as the minimum return a project must earn — otherwise it's destroying value rather than creating it.
The Golden Rule: If ROCE > WACC, the project creates value → Accept it. If ROCE < WACC, the project destroys value → Reject it.
Why Debt is Cheaper Than Equity (After Tax)
🧾 Interest is tax-deductible. When a company pays interest on a loan, the government effectively subsidises part of it — because interest reduces taxable income, which reduces tax paid. This "tax shield" makes debt cheaper than its headline rate.

After-Tax Cost of Debt = Interest Rate × (1 − Tax Rate)
Example: 10% loan with 30% tax rate → real cost = 10% × 0.70 = 7%. The government pays the other 3%.
WACC Calculation — Step by Step (Class Example)
WACC Formula
WACC = (Debt Weight × After-Tax Debt Cost) + (Equity Weight × Equity Cost)
Weights = proportion of each in total capital. After-tax debt cost = rate × (1 − tax rate).

Operating DuPont — Separating Operations from Finance
The Problem with Traditional DuPont
🔍 The 3-factor DuPont (PM × TATO × EM) mixes two different things: how well the business operates (PM and TATO) and how the business is financed (EM). This is a problem when comparing two companies — one may look better simply because it borrowed more, not because it operates better. The solution: separate them cleanly.
The Operating vs. Financial Split
⚙️ Operating Assets
Assets directly used in running the business — inventory, receivables, plant, equipment. Exclude cash and investments held for financial reasons.
ITC operating assets = Total Assets minus excess cash and short-term investments not needed for operations.
💰 Non-Operating Assets
Assets held for investment/treasury purposes — surplus cash, marketable securities, equity investments in other companies.
ITC holds ₹12,944 Cr in short-term investments + ₹17,620 Cr in long-term investments — significant non-operating assets.
📈 Operating Income
Revenue and profit from core operations only. Excludes interest income, investment gains, and other non-core items.
ITC Revenue from Operations = ₹76,840 Cr (core). Other Income = ₹2,728 Cr (non-operating — investment returns etc.).
📊 Operating PM & ATR
Calculated using only operating income and operating assets — removes financial noise for a pure view of operational efficiency.
Op. PM = Operating Profit ÷ Operating Revenue
Op. ATR = Operating Revenue ÷ Operating Assets
Modified (Operating) DuPont Formula
Operating DuPont
Op. ROE = Op. PM × Op. ATR × Op. EM
Where all three use operating figures only. Op. ROA = Op. PM × Op. ATR (pure operational engine, no financing effect)
Why this matters: It is Operating ROE that creates long-term value for shareholders — not ROE inflated by financial leverage. A company that boosts ROE purely by taking on debt hasn't improved its business; it's just increased risk.

Total value of orders placed on the platform — not Zomato's revenue, but a measure of scale and market reach.

CVP Analysis — How Volume Drives Profit
The Core Idea
📊 CVP (Cost-Volume-Profit) analysis answers the manager’s most important operational questions: “If I sell 500 more units, how much more profit do I make? How many units must I sell just to break even? What if I cut my price by 10%?” CVP links selling price, volume, variable cost, and fixed cost into one unified picture. It’s the financial model that separates hope from calculation.
5 Profit Drivers in CVP
Profit is changed by: (1) Selling price per unit, (2) Sales volume, (3) Variable cost per unit, (4) Total fixed costs, (5) Sales mix.
Change any one of these five, and profits change. CVP tells you exactly how much.
CVP Key Assumptions
Selling price is constant. VC per unit is constant. Total FC is constant. Sales mix is fixed. All units produced = units sold.
These hold within the relevant range. Real-world analysis relaxes them for more complex decisions.
Contribution Margin — The Engine of Profit
⛽ Contribution Margin (CM) is what each unit contributes first to covering fixed costs and then to building profit. It is the fuel that runs the profit engine.
Contribution Margin Per Unit
CM = Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit
Class example: CM = ’30 − ’22 = ’8 per unit. Every unit sold contributes ’8 toward fixed costs and profit.
💰 Revenue (SP × Q) 10,000 × ’30 = ’3,00,000

= Contribution Margin (CM) ’8 × 10,000 = ’80,000
− Total Fixed Costs

Why CM (not profit per unit) is the correct decision basis: At 7,500 units (BEP), profit per unit = ’0. But CM per unit is still ’8. Every unit above BEP earns ’8 of pure profit — fixed costs are already fully covered. Decisions based on CM per unit are always correct; decisions based on profit per unit are volume-dependent and misleading.
Break Even Point (BEP)
⚖️ BEP is the exact volume at which profit = 0. Below BEP: every unit digs you deeper into loss (FC not yet covered). At BEP: FC exactly recovered, zero profit. Above BEP: every extra unit earns CM as pure profit.

Margin of Safety, Operating Leverage & Key Factor
Margin of Safety (MOS)
🛡️ Margin of Safety is the cushion between your actual (or budgeted) sales and your Break Even Point. It answers: “How much can sales fall before we start losing money?” A company operating 40% above BEP can survive a significant sales decline. A company operating only 5% above BEP is dangerously exposed.
MOS Formula
MOS = Actual Sales − BEP Sales
Class: Actual = 10,000 units. BEP = 7,500 units.
MOS = 2,500 units (or ’75,000 in value)
MOS % = 2,500 ÷ 10,000 = 25%

Sales can drop 25% before losses begin. Also: MOS = Profit ÷ P/V Ratio.
How to Improve MOS
Push actual sales away from BEP
1. Lower fixed costs (reduce rent, cut admin overhead)
2. Lower variable costs per unit (better supplier terms)
3. Increase sales volume (marketing, new channels)
4. Raise selling price if market allows
5. Shift product mix toward higher CMR items
Degree of Operating Leverage (DOL)
⚙️ DOL measures how sensitively profit responds to a change in sales. A company with high fixed costs has high DOL — a 10% rise in sales might create a 40% rise in profit (because FC doesn’t rise). The flip side: a 10% fall in sales causes a 40% fall in profit. High leverage amplifies both good times and bad.
Degree of Operating Leverage
DOL = Contribution Margin ÷ Profit Before Tax
Also: DOL = 1 ÷ MOS%. Class: DOL = ’80,000 ÷ ’20,000 = 4. A 10% sales rise → 10% × 4 = 40% profit rise.

🔑 In theory, produce unlimited quantities of your highest-CM product. In practice, there’s always a bottleneck — limited machine hours, scarce materials, limited skilled workers. When resources are constrained, rank products by CM per unit of the limiting factor.

4 Strategies to Improve Profitability (CVP Levers)
1. Volume Maximisation: After BEP, every additional unit earns CM as pure profit. Fixed costs are sunk — more volume means no additional FC, only additional VC. Push sales aggressively once you’re past BEP.
2. Reduce Fixed Costs: Lowers BEP directly, widens MOS, and gives you a safer cushion. Outsource non-core work, share facilities, negotiate leases down, cut discretionary fixed costs in lean times.
3. Reduce Variable Costs: Every rupee saved on VC per unit directly lifts CM per unit and improves CMR. Better supplier contracts, process efficiency, waste reduction. More profit from the same revenue.
4. Raise Selling Price: Most powerful lever when market conditions allow. A ’1 price increase on 10,000 units = ’10,000 extra profit — pure, with no increase in VC. Requires strong brand or differentiation to avoid losing volume.

📉 Depreciation = non-cash; added back in cash flow. Goodwill = acquisition premium. EPS = PAT÷shares.
Session 2 — Profitability & DuPont
📈 ROE = PAT ÷ Equity = Shareholder return. ITC: 20,751÷74,889 = 27%.
🏗️ ROA = Net Income ÷ Assets. All-capital efficiency. ITC ≈ 22.6%. ROCE = EBIT÷(E+D) ≈ 36%.
⚖️ EBITDA = use for cross-company comparisons (not PAT). Strips tax, interest, depreciation distortions.
📊 CAGR = smoothed annual growth rate. (End÷Start)^(1/n)−1. Use for revenue trend analysis.
🔬 DuPont 3-Factor: ROE = TATO × Profit Margin × Equity Multiplier. Each = one lever to fix.
⚙️ TATO low → asset inefficiency → advertise/discount/new products. PM low → cost problem → cut COGS.
🗺️ PM↑ + TATO↓ = capital-heavy (utilities/ITC cigarettes). PM↓ + TATO↑ = retail/FMCG. Both can work.
🌱 Retention ratio b = 1 − DPS÷EPS. Internal growth = (ROA×b)÷(1−ROA×b). Sustainable = ROE×b version.
🚀 Sustainable > Internal growth rate always (ROE > ROA for leveraged firms). Use SGR for LT valuation models.
📅 Index trend: base year = 100. All other years as %. Reveals multi-year direction without size distortion.
⚠️ Ratio rules: same period, same accounting basis, use groups of ratios, min 3 years. Never in isolation.
📖 Always read: Footnotes + MD&A. Numbers without context mislead. Management's narrative matters.
Session 3 — WACC, Operating DuPont & Cases
💰 WACC = (Debt weight × after-tax debt cost) + (Equity weight × equity cost). The minimum return a project must earn.
🧾 Debt tax shield: Interest is tax-deductible. After-tax cost of debt = rate × (1−tax). 10% loan @ 30% tax = real cost 7%.
✅ Project Rule: ROCE > WACC → Accept (creates value). ROCE < WACC → Reject (destroys value).
⚙️ NOPAT = PAT + Interest×(1−tax) = EBIT×(1−tax). Financing-neutral profit for ROCE calculation.
📊 EBITDA is financing-neutral: same whether company has debt or not. ROCE (NOPAT basis) is also the same. ROE is NOT.
🔍 Operating DuPont: Split assets/income into Operating vs Non-Operating. Op. ROE = Op.PM × Op.ATR × Op.EM.
🛒 Ahold vs Tesco: Same ROE, but Ahold wins — higher Op.ROA, shorter cycle, less debt, better growth. Market saw through the accounting.
🔄 Working cycle: Cash → Raw Materials → WIP → Finished Goods → Sales → Debtors → Cash. Shorter = better.
💳 Cash Conversion Cycle = Inventory days + Receivable days − Payable days. Negative = company funded by suppliers (like Amazon).
🏆 3 ROE strategies: (1) High PM + Low ATR = Premium. (2) Low PM + High ATR = Volume. (3) High+High = Dream (Apple). Low+Low = failure.
🍕 Zomato / loss-making firms: DuPont breaks down with negative earnings. Use P/S, GMV, unit economics, CAGR instead.
📐 Which metric, whose eyes: Shareholder → ROE. Manager/banker → ROCE (vs WACC). Cross-company → EBITDA margin. Growth → CAGR.
Session 4 — Cost Concepts & CVP
🎯 Cost Object = anything you want to measure cost for: product, service, department, project, activity. Choose carefully — wrong object = misleading decisions.
📏 Direct Cost = traceable to one product (steel in Baleno). Indirect = shared, must be allocated (plant rent spread across all models).
📈 Variable Cost: total changes with volume, per unit stays constant. Class: ’22/unit. 10,000 units → ’2,20,000; 8,000 units → ’1,76,000.
🏢 Fixed Cost: total constant regardless of volume, per unit falls as volume rises. Class FC = ’60,000. At 10,000 = ’6/unit; at 12,000 = ’5/unit.
🚗 GM Lesson: Committed fixed costs (pensions, leases) don’t fall when revenue falls. High FC structure = high risk. GM went bankrupt in 2009 for this exact reason.
🧮 Contribution Margin = SP − VC/unit = ’30 − ’22 = ’8/unit. Covers FC first, then becomes pure profit after BEP.
⚖️ BEP = Total FC ÷ CM/unit = ’60,000 ÷ ’8 = 7,500 units. Below = loss; above = profit. After BEP, each unit = ’8 pure profit.
📐 P/V Ratio (CMR) = CM ÷ SP = 8/30 = 26.7%. Every ’100 of sales generates ’26.7 toward FC and profit.
🛡️ Margin of Safety = Actual − BEP = 10,000 − 7,500 = 2,500 units (25%). Cushion before losses begin.
⚙️ DOL = CM ÷ Profit = 80,000 ÷ 20,000 = 4. 10% sales rise → 40% profit rise. MOS% = 1/DOL = 25%.
🔑 Key Factor rule: When a resource is limited, rank products by CM per unit of limiting resource (not just CM/unit). Maximise contribution from the bottleneck.
📝 Use CM, not profit/unit for decisions. Profit/unit is volume-dependent (changes with FC spreading); CM/unit is constant. Always the right lever for pricing and product mix decisions.

Memory Hooks
🏠 Balance Sheet — The House Analogy
Your house (Asset) = Bank Loan (Liability) + Your savings (Equity). The balance sheet is just that, scaled up to a company. "What it owns = What it owes + What owners put in."
🪜 Income Statement — The Staircase
Start at the top (Revenue), walk down deducting costs at each step, reach the bottom (Net Profit). "Revenue is hope. Net profit is reality."
📸 vs 🎬 — Balance Sheet vs Income Statement
Balance Sheet = Photograph (one moment). Income Statement = Video (a period). Cash Flow = the bank statement showing actual transactions.
🧮 P/E — "How Many Years to Recover?"
P/E = 25 means: at current earnings, it takes 25 years to recover your investment. Lower P/E = cheaper stock (but check why). Higher P/E = market expects strong future growth. Always compare within the same industry.
🏦 P/B for Banks — "What Are You Buying?"
For a bank, you're really buying its loan book and deposits — the balance sheet. So P/B (price vs. what the bank's books say it's worth) matters more than earnings (which fluctuate with NPAs and provisioning). P/B + ROE = complete picture for banks.
⚡ ITC in 5 Numbers
Assets: ₹92,000 Cr | Equity: ₹75,000 Cr (83%) | Revenue: ₹77,000 Cr | Profit: ₹20,751 Cr | EPS: ₹16.42
→ P/E = 420÷16.42 = ~25.6× | P/B = 420÷60 = ~7×
🔑 The Three Analysis Lenses
1. Time Series = Compare company to its own past (is it growing?)
2. Cross-Section = Compare company to its peers (is it the best in class?)
3. Ratio Analysis = P/E, P/B, D/E, Operating Margin — translate numbers to meaning.
🔬 DuPont — The Car Analogy
ROE dropped? Don't panic. Check three things like checking a car: Engine (Profit Margin — is fuel being converted efficiently?), Wheels (TATO — is the car actually moving, i.e. generating revenue?), Gear ratio (Equity Multiplier — how much leverage is amplifying performance?). Fix whichever one is broken.
🗺️ PM vs TATO — The Two Roads to the Same Destination
Road 1 (Premium highway): High margin, low volume — like a luxury hotel charging ₹20,000/night with few guests.
Road 2 (Volume highway): Low margin, high volume — like a grocery store earning ₹2 per item but selling millions.
Both roads lead to good ROA. Apple iPhone = both roads simultaneously = the dream.
🌱 Growth Rates — The Savings Account Analogy
Internal Growth Rate = growing your savings account using only your own salary. No loans.
Sustainable Growth Rate = growing using your salary + a credit card proportional to your income. Faster, but the debt-to-income ratio stays constant.
Both are limited by ROA/ROE × how much you keep (retention ratio b).

Shareholder? → ROE, EPS, P/E, P/B

Banker? → D/E ratio, Interest Coverage, ROCE, Current Ratio

💰 WACC — The Minimum Bar
Imagine you borrow ₹40 from a bank at 7% (after tax) and use ₹60 of your own money expecting 15% return. Your blended cost = 11.8%. Any investment must earn at least 11.8% — otherwise you'd have been better off not doing it. That's WACC. "The bar every project must jump over."
🔍 Operating DuPont — Peel the Onion
ROE has three layers: Operations (PM × ATR) + Financing (EM). To judge a business fairly, peel off the financing layer. Two companies with the same ROE can have very different business quality — one earns it, one borrows it. Ahold earned it. Tesco borrowed it.
🔄 Working Cycle — The River Analogy
Cash flows through a business like water in a river: Cash → Materials → Goods → Sales → Debtors → Cash again. A fast-flowing river (short cycle) is healthy. A slow, stagnant river (long cycle) means cash is trapped — you need more working capital just to stay alive.
🍕 Zomato — When the Ruler Doesn't Work
Using DuPont on Zomato is like measuring temperature with a ruler — wrong tool. Loss-making companies need different instruments: GMV growth (how big is the market?), unit economics (is each order getting profitable?), P/S ratio (what revenue multiple does the market accept?). Match the tool to the company's lifecycle stage.
🏆 The Three ROE Strategies in One Line Each
Strategy 1 (Premium): "Charge a lot, sell to few" — ITC cigarettes, hospitals, luxury.
Strategy 2 (Volume): "Charge little, sell to millions" — Walmart, grocery retail, FMCG.
Strategy 3 (Dream): "Charge a lot AND sell to millions" — Apple. Extremely rare.
Not a Strategy: "Charge little, sell to few" — a dying business. Exit immediately.
🏢 Fixed vs Variable — The Rent Analogy
Your apartment rent is ’20,000/month whether you work 1 hour or 100 hours from home. Your grocery bill rises with every meal. Rent = Fixed. Groceries = Variable. A factory’s lease = Fixed. Its raw materials = Variable. More production spreads the rent thinner — that’s scale economy.
⚖️ Break Even — The “Zero Hour”
BEP is the exact moment the business transitions from losing money to making money. Before BEP: every unit is digging out of the Fixed Cost hole. At BEP: hole is exactly filled. After BEP: every unit = pure ’8 profit (CM). BEP = Total FC ÷ CM per unit. Simple. Powerful.
🪣 Contribution Margin — The Bucket Analogy
CM fills two buckets in order. First, the Fixed Cost bucket (’60,000 — takes 7,500 units). Once that bucket is full, every extra unit fills the Profit bucket. Before BEP: all CM goes to FC. After BEP: all CM is profit. The bucket size (FC) determines your BEP.
🚗 The GM Cautionary Tale
80 years of dominance. 2008 financial crisis hits. Car sales collapse. Variable costs (materials, hourly labour) fell with volume. But committed fixed costs — union pensions, retiree healthcare, plant leases — didn’t fall. Revenue halved; losses exploded. Filed for bankruptcy 2009. High fixed cost structures are powerful in good times and lethal in bad times.
⚙️ DOL — The Profit Amplifier
DOL = 4 means profit moves 4x faster than sales. Sales up 10% → profit up 40%. But sales down 10% → profit down 40%. Operating leverage is a magnifying glass: it magnifies both good news and bad. High FC = high DOL = high amplification — great on the way up, brutal on the way down.
🛡️ Margin of Safety — “How Wrong Can I Be?”
MOS answers: “How much can my sales drop before I start losing money?” MOS = 25% means sales can fall by a quarter before hitting BEP. Low MOS = fragile business, every decision must be precise. High MOS = comfortable cushion, room to experiment and grow. MOS% = 1/DOL — always check both together.
🔑 Key Factor — Earn From the Bottleneck
When you can’t produce everything, the question is not “which product has the highest CM?” but “which product earns the most CM per unit of the scarce resource?” Product A: ’720 CM using 3 machine hours = ’240/hr. Product B: ’400 CM using 1 hour = ’400/hr. With limited machine hours, make Product B. The bottleneck dictates strategy.
