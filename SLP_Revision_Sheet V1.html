Session — Corporate Communication

Crisis Communication & Corporate Responses

Prof. Ranjan Kumar · Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT)

A · Core Concept Summary
  • Corporate communication spans both internal (employees) and external (public, regulators, media) audiences — and crises affect all of them simultaneously.
  • A crisis is not the event itself — it is the stakeholder perception of an unpredictable event threatening expectations and organizational performance.
  • SCCT (Situational Crisis Communication Theory) says the right response strategy depends on how much the organization is seen as responsible or blameworthy.
  • Crisis response must move through four stages: Prevention → Preparation → Response → Revision.
  • Rhetoric — the art of persuasion — underpins all crisis messaging. Aristotle's four appeals guide what you say and how you say it.
📊 Visual — Slide 2

The Scope of Corporate Communication

Corporate communication is an expanding circle — starting from internal messaging and growing outward to global stakeholder influence.

Key takeaway: Effective leaders manage both spheres — internal trust is the foundation for credible external messaging during a crisis.
B · Key Concepts Explained

What Is a Crisis?

A crisis is the perception of an unpredictable event that threatens what stakeholders expect from an organization — and can seriously hurt its performance and reputation.

💡 Real-world: Johnson & Johnson's Tylenol recall (1982) — a supply chain tampering became a reputational crisis because of stakeholder fear, not just the event itself.

SCCT — Situational Crisis Communication Theory

Before choosing how to respond to a crisis, assess attribution of blame: How much will stakeholders hold the organization responsible?

💡 The key question: Was this our fault? Could we have prevented it? Are we a victim too? Your answer shapes your response strategy.

Three Attribution Questions

Ask these before crafting any crisis response:
1. Can the organization be seen as a victim of the event?
2. Was the event unintentional or uncontrollable?
3. Was the event preventable?

💡 A factory fire due to lightning (uncontrollable) needs a different response than one caused by ignored safety warnings (preventable).

Crisis Response: The 4-Stage Process

This chevron flow (from the slide) shows that crisis management is not reactive — it must start before the crisis hits.

PREVENTION
PREPARATION
RESPONSE
REVISION
Prevention Identify and reduce risk before a crisis occurs — risk audits, safety protocols, culture of transparency.
Preparation Build crisis response plans, train spokespersons, designate a crisis team, pre-draft key messages.
Response Execute the communication plan — the right message, to the right audience, through the right channel, fast.

Key takeaway: Organizations that skip Prevention and Preparation always fumble the Response.

📊 Visual — Slide 5 · Rhetoric Framework

The 4 Appeals of Rhetoric — Aristotle's Framework

The slide features Aristotle (384–322 BC), Alexander the Great's tutor. His framework for persuasion remains the gold standard in crisis communication today. Below is the embedded diagram from the slide, followed by a plain-language breakdown.

🧠
Logos
Logic & Evidence

Use facts, data, and reasoned arguments. "Here are the numbers that show our safety record."

❤️
Pathos
Emotion & Empathy

Connect with the audience's feelings. "We understand the pain this has caused families."

🎖️
Ethos
Credibility & Character

Establish trust through your track record and values. "As a 50-year-old institution, our integrity is non-negotiable."

⏰
Kairos
Right Moment & Timing

Say the right thing at the right time. Timing a statement too early or late can undo all other appeals.

Key takeaway: Powerful crisis communication uses all four appeals together — facts alone (logos) without empathy (pathos) feel cold; emotion without credibility (ethos) feels manipulative.

Principled Negotiation & Decision-Making

Fisher & Ury, Getting to Yes (1991) · Penguin Books

A · Core Concept Summary
  • Stop positional bargaining ("I want X, no I want Y") — it creates deadlocks and damages relationships.
  • Principled Negotiation = solve the problem, not beat the person. Focus on interests, not positions.
  • Most conflicts have more shared interests than you realize — conflict is usually concentrated in a small set of truly opposing interests.
  • When stuck, generate creative options before evaluating any of them (separate inventing from deciding).
  • Objective standards (market norms, law, precedent) break deadlocks without anyone losing face.
B · Key Concepts Explained — The 4 Principles

1. Separate the People from the Problem

Treat people issues (emotions, perceptions, trust) separately from substantive issues (contracts, money, decisions). You can't ignore either.

💡 A salary negotiation gone sour: the manager felt disrespected (people issue), not just underpaid (substantive issue). Addressing feelings first unlocks the substantive conversation.

2. Focus on Interests, Not Positions

A position is what you say you want. An interest is why you want it. Dig one layer deeper — the underlying interests are often compatible.

💡 Two managers fighting over office space (positions) — one needs quiet for calls, the other needs the window for clients. Different interests → creative solution possible.

3. Invent Options for Mutual Gain

Brainstorm multiple possible solutions without judging them. Inventing = generating freely. Deciding = evaluating and committing. Keep these separate.

💡 A procurement dispute: instead of "take it or leave it pricing," brainstorm volume discounts, payment schedules, barter, deferred delivery — then evaluate.

4. Use Independent Standards

When negotiations stall, introduce objective criteria: market value, industry precedent, legal norms, expert opinion, or competing offers.

💡 A vendor insisting on a price — you cite three competing bids and industry benchmarks. The standard, not you, breaks the deadlock.

Evolutionary Leadership

The Baby Boom Meets Gen X & Y · Think Before You Speak

A · Core Concept Summary
  • Great leaders adapt how they communicate depending on the generation they're talking to — same message, different language.
  • Baby Boomers and Gen X/Y have fundamentally different mental models of work, risk, and hierarchy.
  • The wrong phrase can shut down a conversation instantly — small word choices carry generational baggage.
  • Evolutionary leadership = recognizing that leadership itself must evolve as the workforce evolves.
  • Replace dismissal with genuine curiosity — the best cross-generational leaders ask questions, not statements.
B · Key Concepts — The "Think Before You Speak" Framework
📊 Concept from Document

Cross-Generational Communication — Phrase Swaps

The document offers specific phrase replacements for both generations. The pattern is clear: replace statements with questions — it signals respect and curiosity.

Who❌ Don't Say✅ Say InsteadWhy It Works
Baby Boomer "That's impossible — it could never work" "That seems impossible to me. Help me understand how it could work." Opens dialogue instead of shutting it down
Baby Boomer "We've done that before" "Here's what we tried before. How is your concept different?" Honors experience without dismissing new thinking
Gen X/Y "You're too set in your ways" "What from your experience makes you wary? Maybe we can figure it out together." Turns criticism into collaboration
Gen X/Y "You refuse to take a risk" "You clearly see danger I'm missing. Help me understand it so we can overcome it." Treats caution as wisdom, not weakness
Key takeaway: Every "don't say" is a statement. Every "say instead" is a question or invitation. Evolutionary leaders invite, they don't declare.

Why Generational Gaps Cause Leadership Friction

Baby Boomers grew up valuing hierarchy, loyalty, and proven methods. Gen X/Y grew up valuing autonomy, speed, and experimentation. Neither is wrong — they just speak different leadership languages.

💡 A Boomer manager saying "we tried that" signals wisdom to peers but sounds like a wall to a Gen Y direct report.

The Evolutionary Leadership Mindset

Effective leadership evolves as the workforce evolves. You cannot lead a 2025 workforce with 1985 communication patterns. Curiosity, not authority, is the new leadership currency.

💡 Satya Nadella at Microsoft — replaced "know-it-all" culture with "learn-it-all" — a textbook evolutionary leadership shift.
