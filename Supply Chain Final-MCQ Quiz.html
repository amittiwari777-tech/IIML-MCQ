Financial Flow in Supply Chain

Q01
The Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) is calculated as:
ADays Payable + Days Inventory − Days Receivable
BDays Inventory + Days Receivable − Days Payable
CDays Receivable − Days Payable + Days Sales
DDays Payable − Days Inventory + Days Receivable
B is correct. CCC = Days Inventory + Days Receivable − Days Payable. A negative CCC means a company collects cash from customers before it needs to pay suppliers — Apple's CCC of −70.57 days is the extreme example of structural buyer power.
Q02
Apple's CCC (FY2022) was approximately −70.57 days. What does this negative CCC primarily indicate?
AApple takes 70 days to convert raw materials into finished goods
BApple pays its suppliers 70 days before receiving payment from customers
CApple collects cash from customers approximately 70 days before it pays suppliers
DApple's inventory sits idle for 70 days on average
C is correct. A negative CCC means the company receives payment from customers before it must pay its suppliers — effectively using supplier money as free working capital. This offloads the entire working capital burden onto capital-constrained SME suppliers.
Q03
In Reverse Factoring, who underwrites the supplier's receivables, allowing the supplier to borrow at a lower interest rate?
AThe supplier's own bank, based on the supplier's credit rating
BThe buyer, using its superior credit rating to back supplier borrowing
CA government trade finance agency
DAn invoice trading platform that auctions the receivables
B is correct. In reverse factoring (SCF), the buyer underwrites the supplier's receivables — the supplier borrows at the buyer's superior credit rating via a bank. This is a genuine win-win: suppliers get cheaper credit, buyers extend payment terms, banks get low-risk loans. P&G's 2013 programme and Walmart's Supplier Alliance (2009) are key examples.
Q04
Dynamic Trade Finance (DTF) differs from traditional trade finance primarily because:
AThe loan is repaid in instalments rather than a lump sum
BThe interest rate adjusts downward as the order passes each trade process step
CIt is exclusively used for pre-shipment financing
DIt replaces blockchain with traditional bank guarantees
B is correct. DTF (Wells Fargo's "variable trade financing") adjusts the interest rate dynamically at each trade step. As the collateral value rises with each completed step, risk falls — so the rate falls too. This aligns cost with actual risk better than static-rate contracts.
Q05
A supplier sells its accounts receivable to a bank at a discount in exchange for immediate cash. This instrument is called:
AReverse Factoring
BInvoice Trading
CFactoring
DPurchase Order Financing
C is correct. Factoring = supplier sells accounts receivable at a discount to a bank (the "factor") for immediate cash. It is post-shipment financing. The bank assumes the buyer's credit risk. The supplier sacrifices margin for liquidity. Reverse factoring is different — it is buyer-initiated and supplier borrows at the buyer's credit rating.
Q06
Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP) in supply chain finance solve which specific problem?
AThey eliminate the need for banks entirely from trade finance
BThey allow facts (e.g., bank balance) to be verified without revealing sensitive underlying data
CThey automate payment when goods are delivered
DThey convert LTL shipments to FTL for cost savings
B is correct. ZKP is a cryptographic method that allows a party to verify a fact (e.g., a supplier's bank balance) without seeing the underlying sensitive details (identity, transaction records). Skuchain uses ZKP so banks can verify supplier creditworthiness in deep-tier SCF without breaching privacy — solving the visibility-vs-privacy dilemma.
Q07
Smart contracts (e.g., TradeLens, Trusple) primarily eliminate which friction in trade finance?
ACurrency conversion risk between trading countries
B7–10 day manual verification delays affecting 80% of transactions
CThe need for buyer credit ratings in reverse factoring
DExcess inventory at tier-2 suppliers
B is correct. Smart contracts are self-executing digital contracts on blockchain that auto-trigger payment when trade conditions are verified (e.g., goods received). They eliminate manual verification which causes 7–10 day delays in 80% of transactions. Examples: TradeLens (Maersk+IBM), Trusple (AntChain), Skuchain EC3.
Q08
According to BIS (2022), trade finance accounts for approximately what share of global exports?
A$18 trillion of $22 trillion (~82%)
B$5 trillion of $22 trillion (~23%)
C$22 trillion of $30 trillion (~73%)
D$10 trillion of $22 trillion (~45%)
A is correct. Trade finance accounts for over $18 trillion of $22 trillion in global exports (BIS, 2022) — approximately 82%. This underscores how central buyer intermediation in supplier financing is to global trade efficiency.

Information Sharing in Supply Chain

Q09
In the CHEAP-B framework, what does the letter "C" stand for as a barrier to information sharing?
AComplexity of data systems
BCoopetition — simultaneous cooperation and competition
CCost of sharing information
DChannel translation latency
B is correct. CHEAP-B = Coopetition · Hidden (multi-chain) · Equity/Fairness · Asymmetric Cheap Talk · Power (Bargaining) · Breach/Security. Coopetition — where a partner is simultaneously a supplier AND a competitor (e.g., Sony to Dell) — creates a genuine barrier: sharing demand data could hand a competitive advantage to a rival.
Q10
Cisco's $2.2 billion inventory write-off in 2001 is cited as the canonical example of:
ACoopetition leading to competitive intelligence theft
BCheap talk — inflated, non-binding retailer forecasts triggering massive over-production
CA supply chain visibility failure due to ERP limitations
DMarket mediation cost exceeding physical cost for a functional product
B is correct. Cisco's $2.2B write-off is the canonical example of cheap talk — forecasts that are costless, non-binding, and non-verifiable. Retailers inflated orders (soft orders cancellable at no cost); Cisco manufactured to those inflated signals; when actual demand was lower, billions in inventory became worthless. Pervasive in electronics, semiconductors, and medical equipment.
Q11
Information Rent refers to:
AThe monthly fee paid by suppliers to access a platform's data portal
BExtra profit earned by a party that possesses superior private information
CThe cost of implementing ERP across supply chain partners
DRevenue sharing between platform and third-party sellers
B is correct. Information rent = extra profit earned by possessing superior private information. Retailers can only be rationally induced to share demand data if they are compensated for this rent. Otherwise, sharing reduces their profit advantage — making non-sharing a fully rational (not malicious) choice. This explains why moral appeals fail; only structural coordinating contracts work.
Q12
When retailers deliberately inflate orders during a supply shortage to receive a larger allocation, this behaviour is called:
ADemand amplification
BShortage gaming
CCoopetition signalling
DDynamic trade finance
B is correct. Shortage gaming = buyers deliberately inflate orders during scarcity to receive a larger proportional allocation. This makes demand signals meaningless upstream, triggering bullwhip-like over-production. It is a deliberate manipulation, unlike demand amplification which is an emergent structural effect.
Q13
The Warner-Lambert + Walmart information sharing experiment resulted in Walmart's in-stock level for Listerine improving from:
A70% to 90%
B85% to 98%
C60% to 85%
D90% to 99.5%
B is correct. Warner-Lambert shared demand forecasts 6 months in advance for Listerine. Walmart's in-stock level improved from 85% to 98%, while Warner-Lambert also benefited from smoother production scheduling. This is the textbook example of successful voluntary information sharing with mutual gains.
Q14
According to the course, the only sustainable solution to information withholding in supply chains is:
AMandatory government regulation requiring data disclosure
BAppeals to cooperation and relationship trust
CProperly designed coordinating contracts that align individual incentives with chain profit
DPlatform ownership of all SC data
C is correct. Coordinating contracts (revenue sharing, buyback contracts, quantity flexibility) are the structural solution — they make sharing individually rational for all parties. Moral appeals ("cooperate for the good of the chain") are not sustainable because they fight against rational self-interest. The contracts must make sharing the dominant strategy.
Q15
Which of the following best describes Coopetition?
AA supply chain strategy combining cooperation across all tiers
BA situation where a partner simultaneously cooperates in the supply chain and competes in the market
CThe practice of two competitors jointly developing a new product
DA pricing strategy that matches competitor prices in real time
B is correct. Coopetition = simultaneous cooperation and competition. Sony is both a supplier (components) AND a competitor (electronics products) to Dell. This makes coopetition the most complex SC relationship — sharing demand data helps coordinate the supply chain but risks handing competitive intelligence to a direct rival.
Q16
Information is described as the "glue" binding supply chain drivers. Which of the following is NOT listed as one of the core SC drivers that information connects?
ASourcing and Pricing
BInventory and Transportation
CMarketing and Branding
DFacilities
C is correct. The SC drivers that information connects are: Sourcing, Inventory, Transportation, Pricing, and Facilities. Marketing and Branding are not listed as SC drivers in this framework — they belong to the demand generation side, not the supply chain structure.

Managing Supply Chain Operations

Q17
The three supply chain revolutions evolved in this order:
AToyota lean/Keiretsu → Ford vertical integration → Virtual global customisation
BFord vertical integration → Toyota lean/Keiretsu → Virtual global customisation
CVirtual global customisation → Ford vertical integration → Toyota lean/Keiretsu
DFord vertical integration → Virtual global customisation → Toyota lean/Keiretsu
B is correct. Revolution 1: Ford's vertically integrated, single-product model. Revolution 2: Toyota's lean, Keiretsu-networked variety model. Revolution 3: Today's virtually integrated global customisation model. Each revolution expanded product variety while reducing the ownership footprint required to deliver it.
Q18
In Toyota's Keiretsu system, Toyota owns approximately what percentage of Toyoda Gosei (a key supplier)?
A9.1%
B23.51%
C42.8%
D51%
C is correct. Toyota's Keiretsu stakes: Denso (9.1%), Aisin (23.51%), Toyoda Gosei (42.8%). The cross-shareholding creates long-term loyalty and information sharing. Japanese suppliers contribute 51% of engineering vs only 14% in the US system — and only 1 in 6 Japanese auto projects are delayed vs 1 in 2 in the US.
Q19
The 3Ts of supply chain disruption are:
ATrade, Technology, and Transportation
BTariffs, Terrorism, and Tsunamis
CTime, Trust, and Transparency
DTaxes, Transit delays, and Technology risk
B is correct. 3Ts = Tariffs (geopolitical/trade), Terrorism (man-made), Tsunamis (natural disasters). The US-China trade war caused customs delays of 3–5 days (normal) up to 15 days (intensive examination). Russia-Ukraine disrupted neon gas critical to semiconductor manufacturing. These represent the three permanent categories of external SC risk.
Q20
In Japan vs the US automotive industry, the number of employee suggestions per year was approximately:
AJapan 2.9 suggestions vs US 0.4 suggestions
BJapan 61.6 suggestions vs US 0.4 suggestions
CJapan 0.2 days inventory vs US 2.9 days inventory
DJapan 14% supplier engineering vs US 51% supplier engineering
B is correct. Japan achieved 61.6 employee suggestions/year vs US 0.4 — empirical proof that lean/kaizen principles create a culture of continuous improvement. Japan also achieved 0.2 days of inventory vs 2.9 days in the US. These numbers demonstrate measurable competitive advantage from lean principles.
Q21
In a postponement strategy, the push-pull boundary is deliberately set so that:
AAll production happens before any customer demand signal
BFinal customisation or differentiation is delayed until after a customer order is received
CInventory is pushed through the entire chain with no demand signal
DSuppliers are paid only after final customers make a purchase
B is correct. Postponement delays final customisation/differentiation to after the customer order — reducing inventory risk while maintaining responsiveness. Quincy Apparel used this: garments taken 80% to completion at factories, final 20% (fit customisation) done post-order. The risk: returned custom garments become stranded inventory with near-zero resale value.
Q22
Which of the following is one of the 9 key challenges of managing supply chains?
ANegative Cash Conversion Cycles
BFragmented Ownership
CInformation rent extraction
DZero-knowledge proof limitations
B is correct. The 9 SC challenges are: Complexity · Conflicting Objectives · Product Proliferation · Shrinking Life Cycles · Changing Technology · Fragmented Ownership · Globalization · Disruptions (3Ts) · Sustainability. Fragmented Ownership (6) is one of them — the modern SC crosses many companies' boundaries, making coordination inherently hard.
Q23
In a pull system, production is triggered by:
AForecasted demand from supply chain partners upstream
BActual customer demand signals
CAnnual production targets set by the manufacturer
DSupplier capacity availability
B is correct. In a pull system, production is triggered by actual customer demand signals — not forecasts. This eliminates overproduction and excess inventory. A push system, by contrast, uses forecasts and produces in advance. Most modern SC strategies combine both: push upstream (where demand is predictable) and pull downstream (where demand is variable).
Q24
Russia's invasion of Ukraine is cited in the course as an example of which type of SC disruption, and for which product/material?
ATariff disruption — disrupting wheat supply chains
BTsunami disruption — disrupting coastal port operations
CTerrorism disruption — disrupting neon gas supply for semiconductors
DTerrorism/Tariff disruption — disrupting neon gas supply for semiconductors
D is correct. Russia-Ukraine disrupted neon gas supply — neon is critical for semiconductor manufacturing (used in laser lithography). This is classified under the Terrorism/geopolitical category of the 3Ts. Ukraine was responsible for a significant share of global neon gas production, making it a critical single-source vulnerability in the semiconductor supply chain.

Supply Chain Visibility

Q25
The 4 types of latency that make supply chain visibility difficult are:
AData · Network · Semantic · Channel Translation
BFinancial · Operational · Strategic · Tactical
CSupplier · Manufacturer · Distributor · Retailer
DERP · IoT · Blockchain · AI latency
A is correct. The 4 latency types (memory hook: "DNS Channel"): Data Latency (IT processing delay) + Supply Network Latency (physical/info movement delay) + Semantic Reconciliation (mismatched data definitions across partners) + Channel Translation Latency (cross-partner alignment delay). Together they create poor visibility → poor decisions.
Q26
According to the SC Visibility document, the real barrier to supply chain visibility is:
AInsufficient investment in ERP systems
BLack of IoT sensors and 5G connectivity
COrganisational silos — not technology
DVenture capital buyouts of network providers
C is correct. "Supply chain visibility is not a technology problem — it is an organisational problem." Each function has different data structures embedded in siloed processes, creating misalignment that no ERP can fix. Two decades of IT investment in ERP has clearly shown it is not the answer. New thinking, real-time architectures, and cross-functional alignment are needed.
Q27
The CINS mnemonic for the 4 Lessons of SC Visibility stands for:
AConnect · Integrate · Navigate · Standardise
BClear (map black holes) · Interoperability over integration · Network stagnation · Solve with new thinking
CCentralise · Integrate · Notify · Share
DCollaborate · Identify · Navigate · Synchronise
B is correct. CINS = Clear (form cross-functional team, map supply chain black holes, size the prize) · Interoperability over integration (tight integration = fragility) · Network stagnation (VC buyouts stalled innovation — 13 networks operate as islands) · Solve with new thinking (not ERP — invest in real-time analytics and event-driven architectures).
Q28
Why is Interoperability preferred over Integration for supply chain visibility?
AIntegration is more expensive to implement than interoperability
BTight integration increases fragility; interoperability achieves data sync with minimal coupling
CERP systems do not support integration across companies
DInteroperability eliminates the need for data standards
B is correct. "Tight integration increases fragility and decreases resilience." Interoperability focuses on data synchronisation and harmonisation — preserving the semantic layer — without tightly coupling processes. This means a change in one partner's system doesn't cascade as a failure across the chain. Resilience requires loose coupling, not tight integration.
Q29
The term "Internet of Things" was coined by Kevin Ashton in which year?
A1995
B1999
C2003
D2007
B is correct. Kevin Ashton coined "Internet of Things" in 1999. Combined with 5G and real-time analytics, IoT is finally making genuine supply chain visibility a practical reality. The 4IR (Fourth Industrial Revolution) = interconnectivity + smart automation, and it is this combination — not ERP — that is driving next-generation visibility.
Q30
The 3 key functionalities of a supply chain visibility solution (IST) are:
AIntegrate · Synchronise · Track
BIdentify & Resolve exceptions · Simplify & Automate workflows · Track & Trace compliance
CInventory visibility · Shipment tracking · ERP integration
DInteroperability · Standardisation · Transparency
B is correct. IST = Identify & Resolve (identify exceptions proactively, allow managers corrective action in real time) · Simplify & Automate (streamline problem resolution via predefined workflows and business rules) · Track & Trace (automate compliance and regulatory requirements across geographies).
Q31
Why has innovation in supply chain multi-tier networks stalled over the last decade?
ALack of demand from enterprises for visibility solutions
BVenture capital buyouts prioritised returns over interoperability, leaving 13 networks as islands
CERP vendors monopolised the market and blocked new entrants
DBlockchain technology proved too expensive to deploy at scale
B is correct. VC buyouts (Ariba→SAP 2012, GT Nexus→Infor 2015, Coupa→Thoma Bravo 2022, etc.) stalled innovation. 13 primary multi-tier networks now operate as islands. Despite technological progress, cross-network single sign-on and interoperability remain unsolved. The recommendation: force SAP/Infor to enable cross-network interoperability.
Q32
Semantic Reconciliation Latency refers to:
AThe time taken to physically move goods between supply chain nodes
BDelays caused by mismatched data definitions across supply chain partners
CThe delay in IT systems processing incoming data batches
DThe time required to translate documents into multiple languages
B is correct. Semantic Reconciliation Latency is the delay caused by mismatched data definitions across partners (e.g., "SKU" means something different in one company's ERP vs another's). This is why the course emphasises preserving the "semantic layer" — harmonising what data means across organisations — as the key focus of interoperability over integration.

What is the Right Supply Chain?

Q33
According to the Fisher Matrix, the average demand forecast error for innovative products is:
ALess than 5%
BAround 10%
C40–100%
D20–30%
C is correct. Fisher Matrix: Innovative products have average forecast error of 40–100% (vs functional products at ~10%). This high uncertainty is why innovative products need market-responsive SCs — the cost of getting the forecast wrong (stockouts + markdowns) is very high. Functional products' predictable demand allows efficient SCs to optimise on cost.
Q34
Market Mediation Costs include which of the following?
ACosts of sea transportation and warehousing
BCosts from excess inventory disposal, lost sales, and customer goodwill loss due to stockouts
CCosts of building and maintaining manufacturing facilities
DCosts of lean production and cross-docking operations
B is correct. Market mediation costs arise from supply/demand uncertainty: excess inventory disposal (markdowns), lost sales (stockouts), and lost customer goodwill. These dominate for innovative products. Physical costs (A, C, D) dominate for functional products. The key insight: for innovative products, paying more for air freight to avoid a stockout is rational — market mediation cost >> physical cost.
Q35
Apple reserves air cargo and distribution capacity in advance of holiday seasons. This decision is best explained by:
AApple using a physically efficient SC to minimise transportation costs
BApple accepting higher physical costs to avoid far greater market mediation costs from stockouts
CApple's Keiretsu-style supplier relationships requiring air shipment
DApple's negative CCC enabling it to fund expensive air freight easily
B is correct. "Apple reserves air cargo and distribution capacity in advance of holiday seasons — deliberately accepting higher physical costs to avoid the far greater market mediation costs of a stockout." An iPhone stockout at Christmas = lost sales + brand damage = market mediation cost >> cost of air cargo. This is the market-responsive SC logic in action.
Q36
Zara's supply chain competitive advantage is primarily built on:
AOutsourcing production to low-cost locations in Asia
BVertical integration enabling design-to-store in a few weeks
CEveryday low prices through cross-docking distribution
DDirect-to-customer mass customisation eliminating intermediaries
B is correct. Zara (Inditex) uses a vertically integrated, market-responsive SC that takes products from design to store in only a few weeks — demonstrating that supply chain design is a strategic weapon. Option A describes Walmart's strategy. Option C describes Walmart. Option D describes Dell's direct model. Zara's proximity + speed through vertical integration is the distinguishing factor.
Q37
Which of the following is a characteristic of a Physically Efficient Supply Chain?
ABuffer inventory of parts/products to handle demand spikes
BProduction located close to the customer
CHigh manufacturing utilisation, lean methods, low-cost sourcing
DFast transportation mode (air freight) aggressively used
C is correct. Physically Efficient SC: high utilisation + lean + low-cost = Walmart model. A and D describe Market Responsive SC. B (close to customer) is also a responsive characteristic. Efficient SCs use low-cost locations (China, Vietnam, Bangladesh), sea transport, and minimise inventory throughout the chain.
Q38
The average stockout rate for functional products per the Fisher Matrix is:
A10–40%
B5–10%
C1–2%
D25–35%
C is correct. Fisher Matrix: Functional products have a 1–2% average stockout rate. Innovative products have 10–40% stockout rates. This is because functional products have predictable demand (forecast error ~10%), making inventory management tractable. Innovative products' high variability (forecast error 40–100%) makes stockouts near-inevitable without a responsive SC.
Q39
The SHIM acronym for Innovative Product characteristics stands for:
AScale · High demand · Inventory lean · Margin low
BShort life cycle · High forecast error · Inventory risk (stockouts+markdowns) · Margin high
CSpeed · Hybrid SC · Interoperability · Market proximity
DSupplier flexibility · High variety · Innovation rate · Market responsiveness
B is correct. SHIM = Short life cycle · High forecast error · Inventory risk (stockouts + markdowns) · Margin high → needs Responsive SC. The companion is FILM for Functional products: Forecast accurate · Inventory low stockout · Long life cycle · Margin low → needs Efficient SC.
Q40
Strategic Fit in supply chain management means:
AChoosing the cheapest suppliers regardless of product type
BAlignment between the supply chain strategy chosen and the product type / market characteristics it serves
CMaximising the number of product variants to serve all customer segments
DEnsuring every supply chain partner uses the same ERP system
B is correct. Strategic fit = alignment between product characteristics and supply chain strategy. Misfit = using an efficient SC for innovative products (causes stockouts/markdowns) OR using a responsive SC for functional products (unnecessary cost). "A mismatch is a leading cause of SC underperformance." Getting this alignment right is the central challenge of SC design.

Strategic Fit, 7Vs & Real-World Cases

Q41
The 7Vs framework used to diagnose the right supply chain strategy consists of:
AValue · Velocity · Volume · Variety · Variability · Visibility · Validity
BValue · Velocity · Volume · Variety · Variability · Vulnerability · Validity
CValue · Velocity · Volume · Variety · Variability · Visibility · Versatility
DValue · Velocity · Volatility · Variety · Variability · Visibility · Validity
A is correct. 7Vs = Value (margin level) · Velocity (lead time/selling speed) · Volume (scale benefits) · Variety (SKU complexity) · Variability (demand/supply uncertainty — core driver) · Visibility (SC transparency) · Validity (shelf life — short validity demands lean/pull). All 7 must be mapped before choosing SC strategy.
Q42
In the P&G + Tupperware horizontal collaboration (Project TINA), truck volume utilisation improved from approximately:
A70% to 95%
B50% to 85%
C60% to 90%
D40% to 80%
B is correct. P&G had 95% weight utilisation but only 50% volume utilisation (heavy but compact products). Tupperware had the inverse: light but bulky. Together = perfectly complementary load profiles. Volume utilisation went from 50% to 85%. This is the classic coopetition/horizontal collaboration example — two competitors sharing logistics to mutual benefit.
Q43
A Dark Store in the quick commerce context is:
AA retail store that operates only during night hours
BA small warehouse (2,000–2,500 sq ft) optimised for picking/packing online orders — not open to walk-in shoppers
CAn underground storage facility for high-value inventory
DA distribution centre with no exterior signage for competitive secrecy
B is correct. A dark store is a small, customer-facing warehouse (2,000–2,500 sq ft) optimised for picking and packing online orders — not open to walk-in shoppers. Zepto's hub-and-spoke model uses dark stores within 1.7–2 km of customers. 60–90 sec pack time + 8.5 min travel at 20–21 km/h = 10-minute delivery. Setup cost: INR 25–40 lakh per store, 13–14 staff.
Q44
India 1, as defined in the quick commerce context, represents approximately what share of India's population and consumption?
A~30% of population, consumes 1.5× the rest
B~10% of population, consumes 3× more than India 2+3 combined
C~20% of population, contributes 50% of GDP
D~5% of population, equal in consumption to India 2
B is correct. India 1 = ~120 million people (<10% of India's total population) who consume 3× more than the rest of the country combined. India 1 GDP alone is ~$1.3 trillion. They are convenience-sensitive, NOT price-sensitive — willing to pay ₹7 more for Thums Up on Blinkit + delivery fee = 18% premium for 10-minute delivery. Quick commerce is India 1 infrastructure.
Q45
Kulicke & Soffa products sit in which quadrant of the Product-SC Strategy matrix, and what does this demand?
AQuadrant A — Efficient SC, push/lean production
BQuadrant C — Efficient SC with some flexibility for supply variability
CQuadrant D — Responsive, flexible SC with buffer capacity (innovative product + evolving supply)
DQuadrant B — Responsive SC but with stable supply side
C is correct. K&S products are Quadrant D: innovative product (high demand variability from semiconductor industry cycles) + evolving supply side (complex semi-finished/finished split, labour turnover risk if relocating). This demands a responsive, flexible SC with buffer capacity and customer proximity — which is why China (close to 83% of demand in Asia) was the winning choice.
Q46
Blinkit's Average Order Value (AOV) leadership over Zepto is primarily explained by:
ABlinkit having faster delivery times than Zepto
BBlinkit stocking high-ASP categories (iPhones, electronics) in dark stores — deliberate category mix strategy
CBlinkit operating in more cities than Zepto
DBlinkit charging higher delivery fees than Zepto
B is correct. Blinkit's AOV (₹635 vs Zepto's ₹450) reflects a deliberate SC strategy to stock high-ASP categories in dark stores. "Category mix is supply chain strategy at the shelf level." One iPhone delivery = equivalent margin of ~110 grocery orders. Zepto wins on speed; Blinkit wins on AOV. Speed is the door-opener; category depth is the margin maker.
Q47
The Zone of Strategic Fit diagram shows that:
AAll supply chains should aim to be maximally efficient
BHigh-variability products need highly responsive SCs; low-variability products need efficient SCs — gaps represent strategic misfits
CA supply chain must be either fully efficient or fully responsive — no middle ground
DResponsiveness always beats efficiency as the source of competitive advantage
B is correct. The Zone of Strategic Fit is a diagonal band on the Variability vs Responsiveness chart. Products above the zone: SC is too efficient for their variability (stockouts). Products below the zone: SC is too responsive for their variability (excess cost). The goal is to stay inside the band — matched SC strategy to product variability level.

Case Studies

Quincy Apparel & Kulicke & Soffa

Q48
Quincy Apparel's primary supply chain failure was best described as:
AChoosing the wrong product category — women's workwear had no market
BA strategic misfit — market-responsive CVP built on an outsourced, low-scale SC with no supplier relationships
COver-investment in technology at the expense of product development
DFailing to raise any venture capital funding before launch
B is correct. Quincy's competitive strategy (affordable targeted fit = responsive CVP) required a market-responsive SC. But their supply chain was outsourced, low-scale, with no factory relationships — a classic strategic misfit. The CVP was compelling (50%+ trunk show conversion, WSJ/NYT/FT coverage, 39% repeat purchase). The failure was entirely operational. They raised $950K vs a $1.2–1.5M target, had 35% return rate (68% due to poor fit), and a 7-week cash runway at the final board meeting.
Q49
In the Kulicke & Soffa case, the Israel + China option was chosen over all alternatives. Which of the following best explains why China dominated the non-cost scorecard?
AChina scored +6 across all 6 non-cost factors: labour, tax, location, positive externalities, infrastructure, and political risk
BChina was the only option with a manufacturing cost below $1/unit
CChina offered the shortest lead time to US customers
DChina's Suzhou park had an existing wire bonding cluster K&S could join
A is correct. China scored +6 on all 6 non-cost factors: labour availability, tax incentives, geographic proximity (close to 83% of demand in Asia + Shanghai), positive externalities (industrial ecosystem), infrastructure (reliable), and political risk (manageable). Singapore scored +3; Jordan −1; Israel 0. Combined with being the cheapest option ($37.27M vs Israel-only $50.85M), China was unambiguously the dominant choice.
Q50
Which of the following cross-document synthesis statements is most accurate?
AERP investment solves information latency; Blockchain solves financial flow; Strategic fit solves operational complexity
BProduct Flow (Lean) + Info Flow (Sharing+Visibility) + Financial Flow (SCF) + Right SC Strategy = SC Competitive Advantage
CResponsive supply chains are always superior to efficient supply chains regardless of product type
DIntegration of processes is more important than synchronisation of data for achieving supply chain visibility
B is correct. The master synthesis: Product Flow (Lean Ops, Doc 3) + Info Flow (Sharing + Visibility, Docs 2 & 4) + Financial Flow (SCF, Doc 1) + Right SC Strategy (Efficient vs Responsive, Docs 5 & 6) = SC Competitive Advantage. All five documents converge on this. A is wrong (ERP is not the answer per Doc 4). C is wrong (strategic fit determines which is superior). D is wrong (interoperability > integration per Doc 4).
